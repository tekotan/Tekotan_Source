Work Experience

Career Exploration

During my time in the engineering program at Dublin High School, I have met a variety of industry professionals through workplace field trips and guest speakers. These experiences have opened my eyes to the true nature of a career in engineering and computer science, and have impressed upon me the importance of seemingly prosaic excercises in my engineering classes.

Field Trips

UC Berkeley's EECS Day

Visiting UC Berkely for EECS day was extremely eye-opening. The field trip involved a complete tour of the school, several keynote speakers that were professors at Berkeley, and the opportunity for conversation with several undergraduate students in the major. One of the most helpful experiences during that trip was a workshop conducted in a typical computer science lab, in which we were taken through what an actual lab would look like in EECS, which gave a very accurate notion of what college would be like. I remember being amazed at the projects the students made for their classes and the massive level of resources available to them, from high-precision robotics to super-fast computers. I was able to experience what it was like to major in electrical engineering and computer science through classroom tours and live demos of the projects students made. It was also helpful to see the lifestyles of the students and how despite being really big schools, there were several quirks about the school that made it feel homely and welcoming to newer students. The experience eventually led me to pay attention to these details when choosing to apply to universities and helped me write my college essays as well

Patriot Jet Team Field Trip

The field trip to Patriot foundation was a very interesting learning experience in which we learned about the planes and their basic structure, learned about supersonic jets and orbital mechanics, and got to spend time in the flight simulators to learn about what it is actually like to fly a plane. The most interesting portion about this field trip was the Supersonic Jet presentation in which I learned about how humans created planes that could go supersonic in level flight and the challenges associated with it. It was interesting to learn about how the amount of drag force on the plane increased so sharply around the speed of sound, and how different techniques such as swept wings and the area rule were employed to reduce the drag, advancements that carried over to subsonic flights as well to increase efficiency. The flight simulators helped me figure out that flying a plane is much harder than driving a car because there is an entirely extra dimension of control you need to keep in mind. Overall, the field trip helped me understand many questions I had about flying aircraft and opened my eyes to an alternate career path.

LAM Research Field Trip

Through an event called the Manufacturing Day, we went to companies like LAM where I got to see how the machinery that manufactures silicon chips was made. On this field trip, I learned about the complexities of managing large teams and dealing with influential and difficult customers. It was very interesting to meet with the different engineers and managers and to hear about their journey through their careers, and the opportunity to learn about their mistakes was in my opinion priceless. Seeing the number of regulations surrounding the cleanroom and the experience of suiting up to enter the room was very exciting, and observing the etching machines and masks in person gave me a new appreciation for the technology around us.

Workday Field Trip

During freshman year, I went on a workplace field trip to Workday, where I met several web developers, project managers, and computer programmers. The trip involved a full tour of the office in which I was able to see the working conditions of all the employees, and things like the notice boards and break rooms. It was super interesting to see how they all went along with their everyday lives and to see a common workplace climate where everyone was mutually acquainted with each other. I also witnessed a live scrum call and was able to look at the GitHub pull request system they had to manage the work of hundreds of employees. The most interesting thing I learned, however, was through the presentations the different employees gave about their backgrounds and how they got the job; they all had unique circumstances from different countries and family backgrounds and yet they all coalesced in one place at Workday.

Guest Speakers

Jason Yosinski

Of all of the guest speakers I’ve listened to throughout my time in the engineering program, one was particularly interesting due to the fact that he was a professional in the field I want to go into, Deep Learning. Jason, from Uber Labs, helped reveal the true potential of Deep Learning to me. Some key takeaways I had from the presentation was how Deep Learning was extremely powerful in the tasks that humans are good at, such as classification and empirical decision making but are not as powerful in tasks such as memorizing data or doing heavy calculations, similar to humans. Additionally, Deep Learning models develop surprisingly understandable abstractions for tasks such as image classification, for example developing the ability to recognize facial features and larger objects and entities in the image itself. The way that Jason was able to apply Deep Learning within Uber inspired me and showed me the immense amount of potential that Deep Learning has to revolutionize all current industries similar to the way computing changed the world.

James Chinn

Another guest speaker we had was James Chinn from Nasa’s Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL). He worked on the Perseverance rover launched in 2020 to land on Mars. His depth of experience in the field of aerospace engineering. After years of watching YouTubers like Mark Rober, I had a significant interest in the work that goes on at Nasa and his talk about the rover and his path to Nasa was fascinating to me. It was also interesting to learn about how he used seemingly mundane pieces of information he learned in high school and college to his job, and how many things that may seem useless when learning turns out to be extremely useful in the long run.

Maynard Holliday

Maynard Holliday was an engineer that worked on a robot that could explore the highly radioactive remains of the Chernobyl power plant. His explanation about the reactor meltdown took place due to the coolant fluid failing to flow into the reactor core and leading the uranium rods to heat up to the point that they melted was fascinating. His speech focused on the difficulties of engineering a robot to work in such extreme environments, as the area was highly radioactive, which would destroy typical electronics and sensors. They also needed the robot to be prepared for the vast number of unknowns regarding what was inside the concrete hull that contained the reactor. The robot’s engineers took inspiration from nature, modeling the movement paths of the robot after animals and insects. Overall, the speech showed me the extremes of the field of engineering and how applicable a lot of skills from school and from various other fields like biology can be sources of inspiration for technological innovation.

Dr. Matt Coleman, Dr. Mattias Frank, Dr. David Loftus, Erin M. McKay

The talk was about the medical challenges scientists and astronauts have to deal with when in space en route to a different planet. The challenges are different from the international space station or even the moon because the trip there and back is within a few days, which means that astronauts can be transported back to earth for critical conditions. The issue with this on the trip to mars is that it takes around 6 months to get to Mars, which means that all medical treatments and diagnoses must occur on the shuttle. To diagnose conditions, blood and breath samples are used, with blood being the gold standard in the medical industry. The blood needs to be centrifuged and analyzed, for which microfluidics is used to do so without expensive equipment. Breath analyzers are similar to blood ones in the way that they need to be separated and then analyzed. The key takeaways of the talk were the ingenious devices that scientists engineer to solve problems around difficult constraints such as space, weight, and complexity. Since astronauts don't have space for full-scale bio labs with centrifuges and other equipment, scientists used the field of microfluidics to separate the blood without using more than a few drops of blood and without the space-wasting equipment. The other interesting thing was the way they used simple condensation principles to collect and analyze the breath of astronauts in the E-Nose.

Engineering Mentorship Program with Mr. Hitesh Parashar and Mr. Silvino Cruz

For the engineering mentor program, I worked with Mr. Hitesh Parashar, Co-Founder of Astellent, and Mr. Silvino Cruz, Senior Electrical Engineer at Twitter. My experience with the mentorship program was extremely fruitful, and I was able to confirm and solidify many of my future plans by bouncing off ideas from my mentor, Mr. Parashar. I do my best planning in collaborative circumstances so this program was perfect for that. One specific thing I learned was skills in writing my resume and crafting my online portfolio. The problem action reaction format I learned was interesting and helped me create and make my resume more catchy. Rewriting my resume using this format allowed me to fit much more information in a smaller space. Another meaningful experience I had was when my mentor was talking about future aspirations for people willing to go into the field of AI through academia and transitioning into the industry. It was reassuring to hear that it was a common career path in AI for someone interested in getting a Ph.D. and interested in research. Some of my best memories from the mentorship program were during the mock interviews we did. I learned a lot during that meeting, especially how to organize and present myself in a way that is appealing to interviewers and people looking to hire me.
